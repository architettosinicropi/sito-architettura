Intellectual Journey

A Harmonic Synthesis of
Space, History, and Soul

The professional path of architect Antonino Sinicropi transcends mere construction, evolving into a philosophical and technical inquiry into the quality of living. Rooted in the PAU Department of the Mediterranea University, his work has developed into a holistic vision where heritage restoration and urban regeneration converge toward a single goal: creating spaces that nourish the individual. His Ph.D. in Urban Regeneration and Safety laid the foundation for a career dedicated to safety—not as a restriction, but as a gateway to social well-being and freedom of use.

Digital Archaeology: Photoscan Survey of Baroque Altars, Gerace Cathedral.

His connection to historical memory and the sacred is epitomized by the prestigious intervention at the Cathedral of Gerace (within the "Art and Faith" project). Collaborating with the Diocese of Locri-Gerace and renowned restorer Giuseppe Mantella, Sinicropi explored new frontiers of conservation. Utilizing 3D surveys and Photoscan methodologies, he digitized marble fragments of Baroque altars to coordinate a meticulous virtual reconstruction—a bridge between traditional craftsmanship and digital precision.

His scientific research for Springer, "Healthy Cities with Healthy Streets", redefines urban infrastructure as a "Third Place." This evolution was significantly shaped by his collaboration with architect Antonio Taccone and his tenure as a Visiting Scholar at Northeastern University in Boston. Together, they adapted international Healthy Streets models into operational strategies for the Mediterranean city, where human-centered design acts as preventive medicine for the community.

Northeastern University, Boston: Analyzing Human-Centered Urbanism Models.

Influenced by the botanical sensitivity of Paolo Pejrone, his approach to greenery is structural: a "cultured garden" where biodiversity integrates with NZEB sustainability. Simultaneously, inspired by the Isabella Stewart Gardner Museum, Sinicropi interprets museum spaces as total works of art—a "courtyard of the soul" where light, proportion, and silence engage the visitor in a transcendent experience.

This complexity is fueled by high-profile collaborations, most notably with Architect Antonello Monaco. Within ISAM (Higher Institute of Architecture and Design), their partnership transcends the academic dimension, evolving into a bond of deep friendship and mutual esteem—an elective affinity that has fostered symbiotic growth between the Roman and Reggina schools of thought.

His commitment to inclusion finds fulfillment in UDL (Universal Design for Learning) specialization, applying special pedagogy to spatial neurodiversity: a vision that dismantles sensory and cognitive barriers to make beauty a universal right. Explore the UDL vision →

Today, this methodology guides Strategic PNRR Governance, where he coordinates complex processes that transform European funds into a new architectural and social legacy.
